## Display Board Design

### Physical Requirements
- Maximum dimensions: 48" wide × 108" deep × 108" tall (verify with your fair)
- Must be free-standing; visible during all hours without electricity
- All text in English
- All graphics and photos individually cited
- No personal contact information displayed
- No raw data visible from subjects (if any)

### Layout (Standard Science Fair Board)

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                    [PROJECT TITLE — LARGE FONT]                     │
├────────────┬────────────────────────────────────┬───────────────────┤
│ ABSTRACT   │           [MAIN FIGURE:            │  RESULTS          │
│            │        Architecture diagram        │  ─────────────    │
│ [250 words │        or key visualization]       │  Main results     │
│ from ISEF  │                                    │  table with       │
│ abstract]  │                                    │  comparison       │
├────────────┤                                    ├───────────────────┤
│ BACKGROUND │                                    │  CONCLUSIONS      │
│ & PROBLEM  ├────────────────────────────────────┤                   │
│            │             METHOD                 │  FUTURE WORK      │
│ Why it     │  [Model architecture diagram]      │                   │
│ matters;   │  [Key equations if relevant]       │  ACKNOWLEDGMENTS  │
│ prior work;│  [Training procedure brief]        │                   │
│ your gap   ├────────────────────────────────────┤  [Mentor name,    │
│            │             ANALYSIS               │  institution]     │
│ RESEARCH   │  [Ablation table]                  │                   │
│ QUESTION   │  [Confusion matrix or ROC curve]   │  REFERENCES       │
│            │  [Qualitative examples]            │  [Top 5 cited]    │
└────────────┴────────────────────────────────────┴───────────────────┘
```

### Design Principles
- **Font**: Title ≥72pt; section headers ≥36pt; body text ≥24pt (readable from 3 feet)
- **Colors**: Maximum 3 colors; high contrast; avoid red/green (colorblindness)
- **Figures**: Every figure has a title and caption; color scale for heatmaps
- **Whitespace**: Do not fill every inch; judges need room to absorb information
- **Key result prominently placed**: Your best number should be impossible to miss

### Tools
- **Canva** (free): Easiest for students; has science fair templates
- **PowerPoint / Keynote**: Flexible; export as PDF then print
- **Adobe Illustrator**: If you want professional-level design
- **Print services**: FedEx Office or a local print shop; 48"×36" poster prints ~$30–60

## The Two-Part Presentation

### Part 1: 5-Minute Opening Presentation
Judges give you ~5 minutes to present before asking questions. Structure:

```
0:00 - 0:30  Hook: "Every year, X people die from [problem] because..."
0:30 - 1:00  Gap: "Current approaches fail because [specific limitation]"
1:00 - 1:45  Your approach: "I propose [method] which addresses this by [mechanism]"
1:45 - 2:30  Key result: "My approach achieves [X%] vs. [Y%] baseline — a [Z%] improvement"
             [Point to your main results table on the board]
2:30 - 3:00  Ablation: "I verified this by removing component A and B..."
             [Point to ablation table]
3:00 - 3:30  Significance: "This matters because [real-world impact]"
3:30 - 5:00  Buffer / questions begin
```

**Practice rule**: Deliver this 5-minute presentation OUT LOUD at least 15 times before your regional fair. Record yourself. Watch the recording. Fix any part that's unclear.

### Part 2: Judge Interview (10–20 minutes)
Judges will probe every aspect of your project. The single best preparation is deep understanding of your own work.

## Judge Interview Drill Questions

Practice answering each of these until fluent. Do NOT memorize answers — understand the concepts so you can answer variations.

### Conceptual Questions
- "Explain [your model architecture] to me as if I'm not in your field."
- "Why did you choose [specific design decision] over [obvious alternative]?"
- "What happens if you change [hyperparameter X]?" (answer with your ablation results)
- "How does your approach differ from [specific prior paper]?"

### Methodology Questions
- "How did you split your data? Why that ratio?"
- "How did you prevent data leakage?"
- "How do you know your result isn't due to random chance?" (p-values)
- "Did you use the test set to tune hyperparameters?" (correct answer: NO)
- "How many times did you run each experiment?"

### Critical Questions (where most students stumble)
- "What's the biggest weakness of your approach?"
- "Under what conditions would your method fail?"
- "Is your dataset representative? Who is excluded?"
- "Is your improvement practically significant, or just statistically significant?"
- "What would you do differently if you started over?"

### Future Work Questions
- "What would you do in Year 2 of this project?"
- "How would you validate this in a clinical setting?" (if medical project)
- "What dataset would you most want access to?"

**The answer that impresses judges most**: Any answer that shows you've thought critically about the limitations of your own work.
