## ISEF vs. Regional Fair: Key Differences

| Dimension | Regional Fair | ISEF |
|-----------|-------------|------|
| Judge expertise | Mixed; many non-specialists | PhD-level domain experts |
| Interview depth | 10–15 min, broad questions | 30–60 min combined, very deep technical |
| Number of judge visits | 2–4 visits | 10–20+ visits (Grand + all SAOs) |
| Presentation standard | Good boards win | Every finalist has a great board — yours must be exceptional |
| Questions | "What does your model do?" | "Why did you choose this loss function over alternatives?" |

**The biggest upgrade needed for ISEF**: Your interview answers must be PhD-seminar quality. Judges will know the literature in your field. They will test whether you do.

## Week 34: Understanding ISEF Judging Mechanics

### Grand Award Judging (Category Judges)
- Category judges are assigned to your specific category (SOFT, ROBO, CBIO, etc.)
- They visit your booth during judging hours
- They are PhD-level experts in or adjacent to your field
- They use the official 100-point rubric
- Multiple judges visit independently; scores are aggregated

**How category placement works**:
- Within your category, judges rank all projects
- The number of 1st-place awards depends on the number of finalists in that category
- A large category (CBIO: 50+ projects) may have 3 first-place awards
- A small category (EBED) may have 1

### Special Award Organization (SAO) Judges
- Completely independent from Grand Award judges
- Visit during separate SAO judging hours
- Judge based on their organization's specific criteria
- You don't know in advance which SAOs will visit you — they self-select based on project relevance

**Key SAOs for AI/CS projects** (prepare for these):
| SAO | What they look for | Award |
|-----|-------------------|-------|
| ACM | Outstanding computing contribution; algorithmic novelty | $4,000 |
| AAAI | Intelligent systems; contribution to AI knowledge | $1,500 |
| IEEE Foundation | Electrical/computer engineering; systems implementation | $10,000 |
| Midjourney | AI/ML projects; creative or generative AI | $5,000 |
| HP | Advancing AI capabilities | Cash |
| NSA | Cryptography, cybersecurity, algorithms | Cash |
| INCOSE | Systems engineering methodology | $1,500 |

**How to maximize SAO wins**:
- When an SAO judge introduces themselves, ask "What aspects are most important for your award?"
- Have a tailored 2-minute pitch for each major SAO (ACM vs. IEEE have different emphases)
- Know the practical applications and industry relevance of your work (SAO judges care about real-world use)

## Week 35: Final Presentation Drill

### The ISEF Interview Mindset
At ISEF, judges are impressed by two things above all:
1. **Deep ownership**: You drove this project. You made every decision. You know why.
2. **Intellectual honesty**: You know exactly where your work is limited and what you would do next.

A common mistake: trying to hide weaknesses. ISEF judges prefer a student who says "My model has a 15% false positive rate on Class 3, and I believe this is because [specific reason], which I would address by [specific plan]" over a student who deflects.

### Final Interview Preparation Protocol
1. Write down 20 hard questions a domain-expert judge could ask
2. For each: write a 3-sentence answer
3. Practice with your mentor (have them play skeptical judge)
4. Practice with a parent or teacher who knows nothing about your field (test clarity of explanations)
5. Time your 5-minute presentation: it must be exactly 4:30–5:00 minutes

### Booth Setup at ISEF
- Arrive early for setup; follow official ISEF setup instructions
- Physical paper copies of your research paper should be available (optional but impressive)
- Laptop/tablet for showing live demos or additional visualizations (check ISEF rules for electronics)
- Printed copies of key figures (some judges prefer to hold something)
- Logbook/research journal visible on the table

## Week 36: ISEF Week (Phoenix, May 9–15, 2026)

### ISEF Schedule Overview
```
Saturday–Sunday    Arrival, registration, project setup
Monday             Official setup day (can work on booth)
Tuesday            Judging Day 1 (Grand Award judges)
Wednesday          Judging Day 2 (SAO judges + Grand Award cont.)
Thursday           Public viewing
Friday             Awards ceremony (Grand Awards + Special Awards)
```

### Judging Day Strategy
- Be at your booth and ready 30 minutes before judging begins
- Maintain energy throughout 6+ hours of judging — judges notice fatigue
- When a judge arrives: stand, greet professionally, offer to present
- After each judge visit: note their name and organization if possible; record any questions you didn't answer well
- Between visits: review your notes; strengthen weak answers

### If Something Goes Wrong
- "I don't know" is acceptable — follow with "but I would investigate X"
- If challenged on a number: don't guess; say "let me show you the data" and reference your logbook
- If asked about a paper you haven't read: be honest; "I'm not familiar with that specific paper — could you tell me more?" Then follow up

## Post-ISEF: Regardless of Outcome

### If you won a Grand Award:
- Write up the experience for your college application
- Consider submitting your paper to a student conference (CSHP, etc.) or a formal venue
- Consider Regeneron STS application for 12th grade

### If you didn't place as hoped:
- Request judge feedback — ISEF provides written comments
- Evaluate: was the result genuinely limited, or was it a presentation issue?
- The experience of ISEF finalist alone is a significant achievement
- Consider building on the project for next year's ISEF or the Regeneron STS

### What to Take Away
A student who completes this 36-week program will have:
- Written a research paper at publication quality
- Developed an original AI/CS project with measurable improvement over prior work
- Practiced presenting complex technical work to domain experts
- Built a relationship with a PhD-level mentor
- Demonstrated genuine research thinking to college admissions

Whether you win a medal or not, this process transforms how you think about problems — which is precisely what top universities are evaluating.
